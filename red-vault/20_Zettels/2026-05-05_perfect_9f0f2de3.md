# **Mentor Observation Heuristics v0.3**

_What a mentor actually watches for, in the moment, without drowning in narrative._

---

## **1. The Three‑Signal Rule**

A mentor only logs something when it shows up as **three independent signals** across time or context.

- **One signal** = noise
- **Two signals** = pattern forming
- **Three signals** = log it in the Jacket

This prevents:

- overreacting to mood
- misreading novelty
- projecting adult meaning onto kid behavior

It enforces **stability before interpretation**.

---

## **2. Behavior Over Story**

Mentors record **what the kid did**, not **why they think the kid did it**.

Examples:

- ✔ “She restarted the puzzle three times without prompting.”
- ✘ “She’s afraid of failure.”

The Jacket is a **behavioral ledger**, not a psychological novel.

---

## **3. The 10‑Second Rule**

If an observation takes more than **10 seconds** to write, it’s too complicated.

This forces:

- clarity
- compression
- operational usefulness

If it can’t be written quickly, it won’t be read later.

---

## **4. The “Would Another Mentor Agree?” Test**

Before logging, the mentor asks:

> “If another mentor saw this, would they describe it the same way?”

If the answer is **no**, it’s subjective → don’t log it.  
If the answer is **yes**, it’s structural → log it.

This keeps Jackets consistent across adults.

---

## **5. The Delta Rule**

Mentors only log **changes**, not states.

- ✔ “He asked for help today; last week he refused.”
- ✘ “He likes working alone.”

The Academy tracks **movement**, not labels.

---

## **6. The “Kid Would Recognize It” Rule**

If you told the kid the observation, would they say:

> “Yeah, that’s true.”

If they’d be confused or disagree, the observation is probably projection.

This keeps Jackets aligned with the kid’s lived reality.

---

## **7. The 80/20 Attention Split**

Mentors watch for:

- **80%**: _process_ (how the kid approaches tasks)
- **20%**: _outcome_ (whether they succeed)

Because the Academy trains **operators**, not performers.

---

## **8. The “One Step Up” Heuristic**

When a kid struggles, mentors don’t rescue — they offer **one step up**, not the whole staircase.

Examples:

- “Try breaking it into two parts.”
- “What’s the first move you can make?”

This preserves autonomy and builds agency.

---

## **9. The “No Fixing During Flow” Rule**

If a kid is in flow, mentors **do not intervene**, even if the kid is doing something “wrong.”

Flow > correctness.

Correctness can be taught later.  
Flow is sacred.

---

## **10. The “Return to Baseline” Check**

After any challenge, conflict, or frustration, mentors watch:

> How fast does the kid return to baseline?

This is one of the strongest indicators of:

- resilience
- emotional regulation
- cognitive flexibility

It’s a core metric in the Jacket.
