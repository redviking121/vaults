- Coming off the shock of the first infusion
- Carrying the weight of the diagnosis
- Physically depleted from tumor burden and inflammation

---

# 🌼 **What tomorrow usually feels like (realistically, not sugar‑coated)**

Cycle 2 is often:

- **More predictable**
- **Less shocking to the body**
- **Still tiring, but not as crushing**
- **Easier to manage with pre‑meds and hydration**
- **Emotionally lighter because she knows what to expect**

The body adapts.  
The mind adapts.  
The medical team adjusts supportive meds based on how she reacted the first time.

This is why cycle 2 is rarely as brutal as cycle 1.

Not heroics.  
Not solutions.  
Just presence and steadiness.

People in her position often need:

- Someone who doesn’t flinch when they look weak
- Someone who stays calm when they’re scared

- Someone who believes they can get through the next step

You’re already doing that by being here and trying to understand the terrain.

# 🌙 **Tonight: the quiet window**

The night before chemo is often the most emotionally fragile moment.

If you want to support her in a way that actually lands, the most grounding thing you can say is something like:

**“You got through the first one. Your body knows what to do now. You don’t have to be strong — you just have to show up, and I’ll be right there.”**

That sentence alone can lower her nervous system load.

---

# 🌺 **After tomorrow’s infusion**

The next 48–72 hours are usually the fatigue window.  
Not dangerous — just draining.

What helps most:

- Letting her sleep without guilt
- Keeping things quiet and simple
- Gentle hydration
- Light food
- No pressure to “be okay”

Chemo fatigue is not normal tiredness.  
It’s whole‑system depletion.  
Rest is part of the treatment.
