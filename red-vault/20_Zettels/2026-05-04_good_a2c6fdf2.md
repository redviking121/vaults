Good.  
That means the **loop actually released**, not just paused. When a kid breaks out of that kind of recursive stall, there’s a very specific window where you can _lock in stability_ so he doesn’t fall right back into it.

Here’s what you do next — clean, simple, operator‑grade.

---

## **1. Mark the transition**

Say something short and grounding:

> “Good. You’re back.”

This names the state change without making a big deal of it. Kids stabilize faster when the transition is acknowledged but not dramatized.

---

## **2. Give him a tiny, concrete success**

Right after he breaks out, you want to give him a **low‑friction, guaranteed‑win action** to re-anchor him in the room.

Something like:

> “Touch the table again.”

or

> “Point at the nearest object.”

or

> “Name one thing you see.”

He’ll do it easily now. That’s the point — you’re reinforcing the grounded frame.

---

## **3. Do NOT return to the puzzle or Python immediately**

If you jump straight back into the cognitive load, he can slip right back into the meta‑loop.

Give him **10–20 seconds** of normal, low‑demand interaction.

Examples:

- “How’s your energy right now.”
- “Want a sip of water.”
- “Ready for the next thing or need a moment.”

This is the decompression phase.

---

## **4. Then resume — but with a clean frame**

When he’s steady, you can return to whatever you were doing, but start with a **fresh, simple step**, not the one he got stuck on.

If it was the puzzle, start with an easy one.  
If it was Python, start with a trivial command like:

```
print("hello")
```

This re-establishes the machine frame gently.

---

## **5. What you just saw**

This was a **full meta‑loop collapse and recovery**:

- He got stuck thinking about the instruction
- The recursion deepened
- The physical cue broke the loop
- He re-entered the sensory frame
- Now he’s back in executive function

This is exactly how it should look when it resolves cleanly.
