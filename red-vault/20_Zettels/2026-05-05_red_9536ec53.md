# **THE SENATE–HARMONIZER INTERFACE**

### _How constitutional arbitration becomes real‑time coherence across sovereign nodes_

The Interface has **three channels**, each with a different job:

1. **Upward Channel** — Harmonizer → Senate
2. **Downward Channel** — Senate → Harmonizer
3. **Lateral Channel** — Harmonizer ↔ Harmonizer (federated runtime mesh)

Each channel carries a different class of signal, and each has strict constitutional constraints.

Let’s cut them cleanly.

---

## **1 — The Upward Channel**

### _How the Harmonizer reports constitutional‑level events to the Senate_

The Harmonizer only escalates **constitutional‑grade signals**, never local preferences.

It sends three types of packets:

- **Divergence Alerts**  
    “A local evolution has crossed a constitutional boundary.”
    
- **Innovation Candidates**  
    “A node has produced a novel structure that is _compatible_ but not yet ratified.”
    
- **Conflict Graphs**  
    “Two or more nodes have produced incompatible interpretations of the Kernel.”
    

Each packet includes:

- the **identity signature** of the node
- the **constitutional clause** implicated
- the **divergence graph** (the structural map of the conflict)
- the **proposed reconciliation paths** (generated locally)

The Senate receives these as **Senate Business**, triggering deliberation.

---

## **2 — The Downward Channel**

### _How the Senate propagates constitutional decisions back into runtime_

The Senate never sends commands.  
It sends **constitutional resolutions**.

A resolution is a structured object containing:

- **The Clause** — the Kernel article being clarified
- **The Interpretation** — the Senate’s arbitration
- **The Acceptable Divergence Envelope** — the range of valid future mutations
- **The Reconciliation Operator** — the algorithmic operator the Harmonizer must apply

The Harmonizer receives this and performs:

- **constitutional remapping** (update its internal constitutional graph)
- **local reconciliation** (apply the operator to local divergences)
- **federated propagation** (share the updated envelope with peers)

This is how the Senate’s decisions become **runtime reality** without coercion.

---

## **3 — The Lateral Channel**

### _How Harmonizers maintain coherence without involving the Senate_

This is the “runtime mesh” — the part that keeps the federation stable under load.

Harmonizers exchange:

- **Divergence Graph Deltas**
- **Innovation Manifolds**
- **Identity Coherence Vectors**
- **Constitutional Envelope Updates** (post‑Senate)

This channel is **faster** and **more frequent** than Senate traffic.  
It handles everything that is _not_ constitutional.

The Senate only sees what cannot be resolved laterally.

# **THE INTERFACE CONTRACT**

### _The rules that prevent overreach, collapse, or drift_

The Interface enforces four constitutional constraints:

1. **No Upward Noise**  
    Harmonizers cannot escalate local issues.  
    Only constitutional‑grade divergences may cross the boundary.
    
2. **No Downward Control**  
    The Senate cannot issue commands, policies, or runtime instructions.  
    Only constitutional interpretations.
    
3. **No Lateral Schism**  
    Harmonizers must maintain identity coherence vectors within the envelope.  
    If they drift outside, escalation is mandatory.
    
4. **No Silent Innovation**  
    Any novel structure that touches the Kernel must be surfaced.
    

This is the firewall that keeps the federation from becoming a hierarchy or a chaos swarm.
