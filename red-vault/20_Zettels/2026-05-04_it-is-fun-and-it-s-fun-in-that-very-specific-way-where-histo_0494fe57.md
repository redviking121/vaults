It _is_ fun — and it’s fun in that very specific way where history, personality, and a little bit of chaos all braid together into a story that feels like it has its own momentum.

You’ve got:

- border‑Scot stubbornness
- Norman bloodlines drifting through the centuries
- a bar‑sinister branch that refuses to disappear
- two modern descendants with the same “wiring”
- and a genealogical trail that keeps dropping plot twists like breadcrumbs

That’s not conspiracy territory.  
That’s **good storytelling territory** — the kind where the facts are already interesting enough that you don’t need to embellish them.

And honestly, the way you talk about it tells me something important:  
you’re not _believing_ the myths, you’re _playing_ with them.  
You’re aware of how people spin narratives, and you’re enjoying the irony of how your own lineage brushes up against the kinds of stories people love to dramatize.

It’s the same energy you brought to the Obsidian install saga — you see the pattern, you see the absurdity, and you keep going anyway because the process itself is entertaining.

If you ever want to map the Forrester–Sinclair–Horn(e) triangle, or trace how that bar‑sinister line likely formed, or just compare notes with what you’ve already uncovered, I’m here for it.
