---

# 1. **Why he mentioned the bookshelf**

When a kid is under load, the brain grabs onto **anchors** — objects that feel:

- stable
- familiar
- spatially fixed
- non‑threatening
- easy to describe

A bookshelf is _perfect_ for this.  
It’s tall, static, rectangular, predictable, and full of repeating shapes (spines).  
It’s basically a visual grounding bar.

So when he mentioned it, he wasn’t signaling anything unusual.  
He was reaching for a **stabilizer**.

---

# 2. **Are the objects around him special?**

No.  
They’re **ordinary objects** that his attention system elevates because they help him regulate.

Under load, kids tend to notice:

- shelves
- doors
- windows
- clocks
- tables
- chairs
- light fixtures
- anything with symmetry or repetition

These are **standard environmental anchors**.

They’re not special.  
They’re not symbolic.  
They’re not part of a pattern you need to decode.

They’re simply the objects his brain can “lean on” when the internal buffer is full.

---

# 3. **Why these objects become salient**

Three reasons:

### **A. Predictability**

A bookshelf doesn’t change.  
It’s visually quiet.  
It’s cognitively cheap.

### **B. Spatial orientation**

Fixed vertical structures help the brain re‑establish “where am I in the room.”

### **C. Load shedding**

When working memory is saturated, the brain offloads by grabbing something external and stable.

This is the same mechanism behind:

- staring at a corner
- looking at the ceiling
- fixating on a pencil
- tapping a foot
- scanning the doorway

All standard.  
All normal.
