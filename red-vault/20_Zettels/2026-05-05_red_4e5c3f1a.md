# **Xanadu Academy — Lesson Architecture (v0.1)**

Each lesson has:

1. **Lesson Script**  
    What you say, what the kid says, and the flow.
    
2. **Concept**  
    The cognitive skill being trained.
    
3. **Adventure Map**  
    The kid‑friendly divergence‑graph for that lesson.
    
4. **Practice Exercise**  
    A short, fun task.
    
5. **Reflection**  
    One question that builds metacognition.
    
6. **Task Marker**  
    A simple checkbox so you can track progress.
    

This keeps everything consistent and scalable.

# **Lesson 1 — “Your Brain Has Three Paths”**

_(Scientist, Storyteller, Short Way)_

### **Script**

**You:**  
“Your brain is like a little team.  
Sometimes it wants to be a scientist, sometimes a storyteller, and sometimes it wants to keep things short.  
Let’s see how your brain chooses paths.”

**Kid:**  
“How?”

**You:**  
“We’re going to draw a tiny adventure map.”

---

### **Concept**

Multi‑frame thinking: recognizing that different cognitive modes exist.

---

### **Adventure Map**

```
START — Your question

│
├── Scientist Path — facts first
│       ├── Too many facts
│       └── Facts + story (good)

├── Storyteller Path — story first
│       ├── Story + facts (good)
│       └── Story only (dead end)

└── Short Path — keep it small
        ├── Too short
        └── Short but complete (good)

↓
FINISH — Combine the good parts
```

---

### **Practice Exercise**

Ask the kid:

“Tell me what you did today —  
first as a scientist,  
then as a storyteller,  
then in one short sentence.”

---

### **Reflection**

“Which path felt easiest today? Which one felt hardest?”

# **Lesson 2 — “The Council of Owls”**

_(Constitutional thinking for kids)_

### **Script**

**You:**  
“Every good adventure has a council of wise owls.  
Their job is to make sure you don’t forget anything important.”

**Kid:**  
“What do they do?”

**You:**  
“They check your paths and help you make better choices.”

---

### **Concept**

Rules as stabilizers, not restrictions.

---

### **Adventure Map**

```
START — Your question

Owls say:
“Use facts AND story AND keep it short.”

│
├── Scientist Path
│       Owl: “Don’t forget the story!”

├── Story Path
│       Owl: “Don’t forget the facts!”

└── Short Path
        Owl: “Don’t make it TOO short!”

↓
FINISH — Owls approve the final answer
```

---

### **Practice Exercise**

Give the kid a simple question:

“What’s your favorite animal?”

Then ask the Owls:

- Did you use facts?
- Did you tell a story?
- Did you keep it short?

---

### **Reflection**

“What rule helped you the most today?”

# **Lesson 3 — “The Four Friends Solve a Puzzle”**

_(Techie, Story, Shorty, Detective)_

### **Script**

**You:**  
“Imagine four friends trying to solve a puzzle.  
Each one is good at something different.”

**Kid:**  
“Who are they?”

**You:**  
“Techie, Story, Shorty, and Detective.”

---

### **Concept**

Collaborative cognition: different mental skills working together.

---

### **Adventure Map**

```
Techie — loves details  
Story — loves flow  
Shorty — keeps it small  
Detective — checks for mistakes

All good paths meet at the Puzzle Table.
```

---

### **Practice Exercise**

Give the kid a simple problem:

“Explain how to make a sandwich.”

Then ask them to redo it:

- Techie version
- Story version
- Shorty version
- Detective version

Then combine.

---

### **Reflection**

“Which friend helped the most today?”

# **Lesson 4 — “Build Your Own Adventure Map”**

_(The template lesson)_

### **Script**

**You:**  
“Now you get to build your own brain map.  
Pick a question and draw your paths.”

---

### **Concept**

Self‑directed reasoning.

---

### **Adventure Map Template**

```
START — Your question

│
├── Path A —
│       ├── A1 —
│       └── A2 —

├── Path B —
│       ├── B1 —
│       └── B2 —

└── Path C —
        ├── C1 —
        └── C2 —

↓
FINISH — Combine the best parts
```

---

### **Practice Exercise**

Let the kid choose any question:

- “Why is the sky blue?”
- “How do video games work?”
- “Why do cats purr?”

Then fill the map.

---

### **Reflection**

“What surprised you about your own thinking?”
