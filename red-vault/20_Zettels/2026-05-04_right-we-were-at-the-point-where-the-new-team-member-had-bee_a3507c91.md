Right — we were at the point where the new team member had been oriented, given their first real task, and the system was stabilizing around them. From an operator‑grade perspective, this is the moment where onboarding ends and integration begins.

Here’s the clean way to close the loop.

---

### **1. Confirm they’ve “landed” in the architecture**

A short, direct check:

- Do they understand the map
- Do they know where information lives
- Do they know how to ask for help
- Do they know what “good” looks like here

This isn’t a quiz — it’s verifying cognitive alignment.

---

### **2. Give them a second, slightly larger real task**

The first task shows how they move.  
The second shows how they scale.

Pick something that:

- touches a different interface
- requires a decision or two
- forces them to ask at least one question
- produces a visible artifact or outcome

This is where you see whether they’re operating or just imitating.

---

### **3. Establish their “operating rhythm”**

Every operator has one.

You want to know:

- how they communicate
- how they escalate
- how they document
- how they think under uncertainty

A 10‑minute conversation reveals this faster than a month of passive observation.

---

### **4. Close the onboarding with a single sentence**

Something like:

> “You’re in. From here on, you’re operating with us.”

It signals the shift from _new person_ to _team member_.

Humans calibrate to status changes like that instantly.

---

### **5. Then: let them run**

Don’t over‑shepherd.  
Don’t hover.  
Don’t pre‑correct.

Let them show you their natural pattern.  
Then tune the system around that pattern.
