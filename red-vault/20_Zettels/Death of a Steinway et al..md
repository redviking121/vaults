Death of a Steinway



Back in the 1950's we lived in an old Dutch Colonial red brick house in Shrewsbury, Missouri. Shrewsbury is a suburb of St. Louis.  The house was from the 1880s, solid brick and still had the gas light fittings in the walls and ceilings.  The upright Steinway in the living room was nearly as old as the house.  It was a big dark mahogany upright and it positively dominated the living room.  It backed up to the stairs that went up to the bedrooms and there was enough space to crawl in back of the Steinway to hide out.  I hid out back there from time to time even when I was not playing hide and go seek.

Grandmother played the piano.  She lived with us.  She and my grandfather had lived there.  He was gone before I was born.  After he passed my parents had moved in with her.  Dad was back from WWII and the Philippines.  Mom and Dad had been married in San Diego.  There were stories about Dad having seen a cigar shaped object over the San Diego Bay in broad daylight during the war. He had been a drill instructor and would not let anyone forget it.  He had me doing the manual of arms when I was 8 years old.

Grandmother would sit with me at the piano from time to time and show me where to put my fingers to play the ebony and ivory keys. One of the tunes where the fingers sort of walked the keys was "Alley Cat".  I would play that for hours sometimes.  This generally upset the entire household. Some of the keys had chips in the ebony.  The ivory was yellowed and chipped with nibbled looking tops.  It was a lot of fun to play with the pedals and strike a key, then listen to the sounds that the piano made inside.  If you put your ear on the piano you could hear all the different vibrations.  The low rumbling notes were particularly fun. They would go on and on and on and slowly fade away.  If an ear was to the Steinway it got softer and more rumbling as it seemed to disappear into the piano itself.

One of our neighbors carried Steinway, Merkel Piano.
As one may guess lot of noise was coming out of the neglected Steinway.  As practice continued "Alley Cat" was actually playing on the piano.  Mom was impressed and had a piano tuner come over.  Apparently the old upright Steinway was one that was notoriously hard to keep in tune.  There was a lot of yelling between Mom and Dad about spending the money to fix the piano.    I hid out in another part of the house with all that going on.  There were peaceful places to disappear into that I discovered over time. Places like under the bed, in a closet or under a dresser that had short legs.  Furniture looked a lot different from underneath.  The wood was not varnished or stained.  There were also pencil marks on it from the cabinet makers from when the put them together.  Sometimes there were even initials.

The back of the Steinway was a lot like the other furniture.  My view of things was a bit different you see.  Guests would comment on the nice finish on the antique furniture and I could see the unfinished wood from below.  Different worlds.

Then there was a nice spring morning.  I was playing "Alley Cat" and Dad came home.  He started screaming at my Mom who was in the kitchen.  "I don't want my son wasting his time at that thing, I want him to do things that will make him a real man.  Your going to turn him into a sissy"!

He marched into the living room. I jumped back from the piano.
Then he pulled the piano out from the wall.  As he rolled it I could hear the insides of the piano thunder.  He pushed it through the kitchen and to the door. Then he tipped it up.  It came crashing down outside and lay on it's back.  Then he rushed into the garage and came back with a sledge hammer.  As he whacked at it.  Pieces of mahogany, ebony, and ivory went flying.
Now the big harp that was the heart of the piano was exposed.
He took a pair of wire cutters and cut the strings one at a time.
Each of them letting out a pained sound as it went to it's silence. I was in shock. That was not enough, now he made me help with cutting the strings.  He said.  "Use your hands and work with tools and you will be a real man."

My grandmother did not say a word about any of this.  It was clear though that she was heartbroken and shocked that her son-in-law would destroy the piano that she and her husband used to play.
At to how the affected me, his efforts were successful. I did get good at using tools and weapons but it just was not a passion. For sure though, this guy did not want to touch a piano keyboard.  It just didn't feel like it was safe for the piano.

Some part of me always has been expecting that it there is something that I really liked that it may be taken away violently.

I still think about that "Alley Cat" and grandma and that old Steinway upright.  Dad has passed now for many years, maybe just maybe there is a piano out there that is up for some dangerous adventures.
