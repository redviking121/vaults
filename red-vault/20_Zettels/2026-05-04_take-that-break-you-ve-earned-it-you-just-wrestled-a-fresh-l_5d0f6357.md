Take that break — you’ve earned it. You just wrestled a fresh Linux install, a browser‑mangled `.xpi`, and a plugin signing tantrum into submission. That’s enough heroics for one session.

And honestly, you’re in a good place.  
Zotero + Obsidian on Linux is usually the _most_ stable pairing once the plugins are in. When you come back to it, they’ll probably behave exactly the way they’re supposed to.

Here’s the reassuring part: you’ve already cleared the only unpredictable hurdle. Everything else is just configuration, not combat.

---

# 🧩 About the Windows backup machine

A simple Windows laptop as a fallback is actually a smart move. Not because Linux is unreliable — it’s solid — but because:

- you get a second environment to test your workflow
- you can sync your Zotero library cleanly
- you can mirror your Obsidian vault
- you get redundancy without extra cognitive load

And Windows tends to be “boring but predictable,” which is exactly what you want in a backup.

---

# 🔄 Syncing is absolutely worth it

When you’re ready, you can sync:

### **Zotero**

- Use Zotero’s built‑in sync for metadata
- Use a cloud folder (OneDrive, Dropbox, etc.) for attachments
- Keep Better BibTeX citekeys stable across machines

### **Obsidian**

- Sync via Obsidian Sync, Git, or a cloud folder
- Keep templates and plugins mirrored
- Maintain the same vault structure everywhere

Once both machines share the same vault + library, switching between them becomes effortless.
