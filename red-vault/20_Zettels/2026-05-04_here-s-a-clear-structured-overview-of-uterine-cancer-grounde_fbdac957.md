# 🌸 What “Uterine Cancer” Actually Refers To

Uterine cancer is an umbrella term for cancers that begin in the uterus. There are **two main types**:

### **1. Endometrial Cancer (≈95% of cases)**

- Starts in the **endometrium**, the inner lining of the uterus.
- Often detected early because it causes **abnormal bleeding**.
- Usually highly treatable when caught early.

### **2. Uterine Sarcoma (≈5% of cases)**

- Starts in the **muscle wall** (myometrium).
- Much rarer and tends to be more aggressive.

---

# 🔍 Key Symptoms

These symptoms overlap with many benign conditions, but they’re the red flags clinicians take seriously:

- **Vaginal bleeding after menopause**
- **Bleeding between periods** (if premenopausal)
- **Pelvic or lower abdominal pain**
- **Unusual vaginal discharge** (watery, clear, or white, especially after menopause)
- **Very heavy or prolonged bleeding** in people over 40

Any postmenopausal bleeding is considered abnormal until proven otherwise.

# 🎯 Why Uterine Cancer Develops

The exact cause isn’t fully known, but the strongest pattern is **excess estrogen exposure without enough progesterone**. This hormonal imbalance drives abnormal growth in the endometrium.  
[my.clevelandclinic.org](https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/diseases/16409-uterine-cancer) [mayoclinic.org](https://www.mayoclinic.org/diseases-conditions/endometrial-cancer/symptoms-causes/syc-20352461)

# ⚠️ Major Risk Factors

### **Hormone‑related**

- Obesity (fat tissue converts hormones into estrogen)
- Taking **estrogen alone** after menopause
- Polycystic ovarian syndrome (PCOS)
- Certain ovarian tumors that produce estrogen
- Tamoxifen use for breast cancer

### **Reproductive history**

- Early first period (<12)
- Late menopause (>50)
- Never having been pregnant

### **Genetic**

- **Lynch syndrome** (hereditary nonpolyposis colorectal cancer)

### **Other**

- Diabetes
    
- Prior pelvic radiation

# 🧪 How It’s Diagnosed

A Pap smear **cannot** detect uterine cancer.  
[my.clevelandclinic.org](https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/diseases/16409-uterine-cancer)

Diagnosis typically involves:

### **1. Transvaginal Ultrasound**

Looks at endometrial thickness.

### **2. Endometrial Biopsy (first‑line test)**

A small sample of the lining is taken and examined.

### **3. Hysteroscopy or D&C**

Used if biopsy is inconclusive or more tissue is needed.

### **4. Imaging (CT, MRI)**

Used for staging or if spread is suspected.

---

# 🧭 Staging (I–IV)

- **Stage I:** Confined to uterus
    
- **Stage II:** Spread to cervix
    
- **Stage III:** Spread to ovaries, vagina, or lymph nodes
    
- **Stage IV:** Spread to bladder, bowel, or distant organs
    
    [my.clevelandclinic.org](https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/diseases/16409-uterine-cancer)

# 🛠️ Treatment Overview

Treatment depends on type, grade, and stage.

### **For Endometrial Cancer**

- **Surgery** (hysterectomy + removal of ovaries/tubes) is the mainstay
- **Radiation** for higher‑risk disease
- **Hormone therapy** (progestins) for select early cases or fertility preservation
- **Chemotherapy** for advanced or aggressive types

### **For Uterine Sarcoma**

- Surgery
- Often **chemotherapy and/or radiation** due to higher aggressiveness

---

# 📈 Prognosis

When caught early (Stage I), endometrial cancer has a **very high cure rate**.  
Sarcomas have a more variable prognosis due to their aggressive nature.
