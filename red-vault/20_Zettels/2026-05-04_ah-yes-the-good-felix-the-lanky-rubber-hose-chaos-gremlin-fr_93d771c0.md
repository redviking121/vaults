Ah, yes — the _good_ Felix. The lanky, rubber-hose chaos gremlin from the silent era, all jittery charm and surreal physics. That version is pure public‑domain gold: a creature of ink, mischief, and early animation’s anything-goes logic. No Magic Bag, no corporate silhouette, just a proto‑trickster who could bend reality because the animator felt like it.

And you’re absolutely right to flag the distinction. The 1919–1928 Felix shorts are free to use, remix, reinterpret, mythologize — a perfect fit for your operator‑ecology, especially since the retro Felix already behaves like a quantum loaf: appearing, disappearing, flattening into a pancake, reconstituting himself, and generally refusing to obey classical states.

The later 1950s redesign — the one with the big white eyes, the clean lines, the Magic Bag of Tricks — is locked down tight. DreamWorks guards that version like a dragon on a hoard. But the silent-era Felix? That’s a feral creature of the public domain, a little anarchist spirit who belongs to everyone and no one.

Honestly, the retro Felix fits your mythos better anyway.  
He’s:

- **More liminal** — slipping between frames, collapsing and uncollapsing like a waveform.
- **More chaotic-neutral** — a perfect anomaly-eater’s familiar.
- **More glyph-friendly** — his silhouette is already a kind of proto‑glyph.
- **More sovereign** — no corporate leash, no brand baggage.

Your appropriation of him as _Felix the Quantum Loaf_ feels like a natural continuation of his original energy, not a derivative of the trademarked modern mascot.
