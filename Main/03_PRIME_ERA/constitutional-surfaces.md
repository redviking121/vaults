Xanadu Prime — Constitutional Surfaces (v1.0)
1. Purpose
Constitutional Surfaces are the highest‑authority documents in the Prime epoch.
They define:

the axioms of the substrate

the governance rules

the lifecycle mechanics

the naming system

the identity and invariants of the architecture

These surfaces cannot be overridden by any other class.
They are the constitutional law of the system.

2. Definition
A Constitutional Surface is any document that:

establishes a system‑level invariant

defines a rule that governs all other surfaces

binds naming, lifecycle, or substrate behavior

constrains evolution across epochs

participates in identity, lineage, or governance

These surfaces form the bedrock of Prime.

3. Constitutional Surface Set (Prime Epoch)
3.1 Substrate Axioms
The foundational truths of the system.
Defines what the substrate is, what it permits, and what it forbids.

3.2 Substrate Lifecycle
The rules governing how substrate components are created, stabilized, propagated, and retired.

3.3 DSR‑1
Decision Stability Rule 1 — the core governance mechanism that ensures decisions are declared, scoped, stabilized, and bound before propagation.

3.4 Razor Principles
The conflict‑resolution and coherence‑preservation rules.
Defines how contradictions are handled (Reconcile, Split, Elevate).

3.5 Naming Governance Clause
The constitutional rules for naming surfaces, directories, epochs, and entities.
Prevents namespace drift and ambiguity.

3.6 Lifecycle Rules
Defines the universal lifecycle for all surfaces:
Declared → Stabilized → Propagated.

3.7 Naming Conventions
The structural naming system for files, directories, surfaces, and indices.

4. Constitutional Invariants
These invariants apply to all Constitutional Surfaces:

Immutable Once Stabilized — cannot be altered without a constitutional event.

Governance‑Bearing — all other surfaces must comply with them.

Epoch‑Transcendent — survive beyond Prime into successor epochs.

Identity‑Binding — define the identity of the system itself.

Non‑Delegable — cannot be overridden by local rules or runtime logic.

These invariants ensure the system cannot drift, fork, or collapse.

5. Constitutional Lifecycle
Constitutional Surfaces follow a stricter lifecycle than normal surfaces:

Declared — name and scope defined.

Stabilized — locked under DSR‑1 with explicit boundaries.

Ratified — becomes part of the constitutional layer.

Propagated — integrated into the Prime corpus.

No constitutional surface may skip a step.

6. Relationship to Other Surface Classes
Constitutional Surfaces sit at the top of the hierarchy:

Structural Surfaces must obey them.

Public Surfaces must reflect them.

Provenance Surfaces must record them.

Operational Surfaces must implement them.

They are the root authority of the system.
