**ai**: This image illustrates the mechanism of **bacterial chemotaxis**, specifically how bacteria like *E. coli* navigate their environment in response to a chemical gradient (in this case, maltose).

### Key Insights:

*   **Chemotaxis:** This is the process by which bacteria move toward higher concentrations of nutrients (attractants) or away from harmful substances (repellents).
*   **Biased Random Walk:** Bacteria do not swim in a straight line toward a target. Instead, they move in a series of "runs" (swimming straight) and "tumbles" (changing direction randomly).
*   **The "Go" and "Stop" Mechanism:**
    *   **"Go" (Run):** When the bacterium senses it is moving toward a higher concentration of the attractant (maltose), it suppresses the tumbling mechanism, allowing it to continue its "run" in that direction.
    *   **"Stop" (Tumble):** When the concentration of the attractant is not increasing (or is decreasing), the bacterium triggers a "tumble," causing it to reorient itself randomly.
*   **Navigation Strategy:** By extending the duration of "runs" when moving toward a favorable environment and tumbling when the environment is unfavorable, the bacterium effectively migrates toward the source of the nutrient over time.

In essence, the bacterium uses a "memory" of its recent past to decide whether to continue its current path or to try a new direction, allowing it to navigate complex chemical landscapes despite its simple structure.
