## 🧬 Overview: How human cellular refresh rates are organized

Human cells fall into a few broad turnover categories:

- **Rapid‑renewal cells** — hours to days
- **Intermediate‑renewal cells** — weeks to months
- **Slow‑renewal cells** — years
- **Non‑renewing or extremely long‑lived cells** — decades to lifetime

This structure is ideal for a visual chart because it creates intuitive “bands” of biological time.

---

## 📊 Major human cell types and their approximate life cycles

### **Rapid turnover (hours → days)**

These cells exist in high‑stress, high‑exposure environments and must be replaced constantly.

- **Intestinal epithelial cells** — ~3–5 days
- **Stomach lining cells** — ~2–9 days
- **Skin epidermal keratinocytes** — ~10–30 days
- **White blood cells (neutrophils)** — ~1–5 days
- **Platelets** — ~8–10 days
- **Taste receptor cells** — ~10 days
- **Lung alveolar type II cells** — ~14 days (variable)

### **Intermediate turnover (weeks → months)**

These cells balance stability with the need for periodic renewal.

- **Red blood cells** — ~120 days
- **Olfactory neurons** — ~30–60 days
- **Liver hepatocytes** — ~300–500 days (but can regenerate rapidly when injured)
- **Pancreatic beta cells** — months to years (slow but not fixed)

### **Slow turnover (years)**

These cells are stable and long‑lived but not completely permanent.

- **Adipocytes (fat cells)** — ~8–10 years
- **Bone osteoblasts/osteoclasts** — ~3–10 years (bone remodeling cycle)
- **Lens epithelial cells (outer layers)** — years
- **Cardiac fibroblasts** — years (slow replacement)

### **Minimal or no turnover (decades → lifetime)**

These cells are effectively permanent and define the body’s long-term continuity.

- **Cortical neurons** — lifetime
- **Cerebellar Purkinje cells** — lifetime
- **Cardiomyocytes** — extremely slow (~1% per year; ~40% replaced over a lifetime)
- **Inner lens fiber cells** — lifetime
- **Oocytes** — fixed pool from birth

## 🧩 How this becomes a visual aid

Depending on your dissemination context, the chart can take one of three shapes:

- **A radial “clock of the body”** — inner ring = fast turnover, outer ring = lifetime cells
- **A horizontal timeline** — left = hours, right = decades
- **A matrix** — rows = tissues, columns = turnover bands

Each format emphasizes different cognitive affordances (speed, hierarchy, lineage).
